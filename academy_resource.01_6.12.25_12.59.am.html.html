Language Through The Mathematical Lens

Just as mathematics can be viewed as a language, language itself can be rigorously analyzed using mathematical tools. From formal grammars that generate sentences to theories that quantify information, mathematics provides a powerful lens for uncovering the hidden structures and principles of human language. This section explores some of the key frameworks and concepts.

Chomsky's Generative Grammar

Noam Chomsky revolutionized linguistics by proposing that language is generated by an innate, computational system in the mind. A core operation in his modern theory is "Merge," which recursively combines elements to build sentences.

NP: the cat
Merge combines two objects (e.g., a determiner 'the' and a noun 'cat') to form a new phrase.

The Chomsky Hierarchy of Languages

This hierarchy classifies formal languages based on their complexity, from simple "Regular" languages to the highly complex "Recursively Enumerable" languages that require a full Turing machine to process. Natural human languages are thought to be at least "Context-Free."

Recursively Enumerable (Turing Machine)
Most complex; recognized by a Turing machine.
Context-Sensitive
More complex grammars. Natural language may fall here.
Context-Free
Can handle nested structures, like phrases within phrases.
Regular
Simplest form, like basic patterns.
